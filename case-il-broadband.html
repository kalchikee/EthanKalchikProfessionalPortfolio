Leaflet FCC / ACS Need Index 3,263 Tracts

Illinois Broadband Gap Analysis

An interactive tool mapping broadband service gaps across every census tract in Illinois — built so that infrastructure planners, grant writers, and community advocates can actually identify where investment is needed most and make a defensible case for why.

Live Project ↗ ← Back to Case Studies

The Problem

The federal government has put billions of dollars into broadband infrastructure through BEAD and other grant programs, and states like Illinois have their own funding layers on top of that. The problem is that deciding where that money goes requires knowing where coverage is actually bad — and the FCC data that most of this is based on is notoriously unreliable. The reason the FCC data overstates coverage is because it reports a census block as covered if even one address in that block can receive service. In rural areas that means a carrier can claim coverage for a large block based on one farmhouse at the edge of their service territory while the rest of that block has nothing.

I built this tool because I wanted something that went beyond just mapping what the FCC said and instead let the user combine coverage data with the demographic and socioeconomic context that should drive investment decisions. Who lives in the gap is just as important as where the gap is.

Methodology

1. Processing the FCC Data

The FCC Broadband Data Collection reports availability at the census block level, but I needed tract-level summaries because tracts are the unit that ACS socioeconomic data comes in. The reason I aggregated up to tracts rather than trying to join ACS data down to blocks is that block-level population estimates from the ACS are too unreliable — the margins of error are huge at that geography. So I summarized FCC coverage at the tract level by calculating what percentage of the tract's blocks had access to broadband at the FCC's threshold speed of 25/3 Mbps for fixed service.

I also separated fixed broadband from mobile broadband because they are not the same thing. The reason that distinction matters is that mobile broadband at the 25/3 threshold is not a meaningful substitute for fixed service for most households — you cannot work from home or do homework on a shared mobile connection the same way you can on a fixed line. I let the user toggle between the two so they can see how the coverage picture changes.

2. Building the Need Index

Coverage alone does not tell you where to prioritize investment. A tract with 40% broadband coverage in a wealthy suburb might be a lower priority than a tract with 40% coverage in a rural area with high poverty and low educational attainment. The reason I built a composite Need Index is to capture both the coverage gap and the vulnerability of the population living in that gap.

The Need Index combines three components: the broadband coverage gap score (how underserved the tract is), the poverty rate, and the percentage of the population without a high school diploma. I weighted broadband gap at 50%, poverty at 30%, and educational attainment at 20%. The reason the coverage gap gets the highest weight is that this is fundamentally a broadband tool — the demographic variables are there to help prioritize among the gaps, not to replace the gap as the primary signal.

Need Index ComponentWeightRationale
Broadband Coverage Gap50%Primary signal — directly measures service deficit
Poverty Rate30%Captures inability to afford service even if available
Population Without HS Diploma20%Proxy for digital literacy barriers compounding access gaps

3. Making the Weights Adjustable

The most important design decision I made was to let the user adjust the weights themselves rather than locking them in. The reason for that is that a grant writer working on a rural infrastructure program has different priorities than an advocate working on digital equity in Chicago — one cares mostly about the coverage gap, the other might weight poverty much higher. By exposing the weighting to the user the tool becomes useful across those different contexts instead of just reflecting my judgment about what matters most.

4. Colorblind-Safe Palette and CSV Export

I used a colorblind-safe sequential palette rather than a standard red-green scheme and the reason for that is a tool like this is meant to be shared in reports and presentations where you cannot control who is reading it. Roughly 8% of men have some form of color vision deficiency and a red-green diverging palette would make the map unreadable for them. I also added a CSV export so that users can pull the tract-level Need Index scores into their own analysis or use them to populate a grant application.

Key Findings

Rural downstate tracts dominate the high-need tier

When I calculated Need Index scores across all 3,263 tracts the highest-scoring tracts were concentrated in rural downstate counties — particularly in the southern and western parts of the state. The reason those areas score so high is that they have both low FCC-reported coverage and elevated poverty rates, which means they score poorly on two of the three index components simultaneously.

FCC data understates the problem in mixed rural-suburban areas

What stood out to me when I dug into the data was how many tracts on the urban-rural fringe had what looked like decent coverage on paper but very uneven distribution within the tract. The reason that matters is that a tract might have 70% coverage but that 70% is concentrated in the denser end of the tract near a highway — leaving a large rural portion of the same tract completely unserved. The FCC's census block methodology makes this hard to see at first glance.

Mobile-only tracts are disproportionately high-poverty

When I filtered for tracts that had mobile broadband coverage but no fixed broadband coverage — meaning residents can technically get service but only on mobile — those tracts had a poverty rate about 1.8x higher than the statewide average. The reason that is significant is that mobile-only households are more vulnerable to data caps and speed throttling, which makes the digital divide in those areas worse than the raw coverage numbers suggest.

Chicago has pockets of need that are easy to miss at the state scale

Zooming into Chicago the tool surfaces several high-need tracts on the South and West sides where the Need Index scores are driven primarily by poverty and educational attainment rather than coverage gaps. The reason those tracts still score high is that even though fixed broadband infrastructure technically passes through them, adoption rates are low because households cannot afford the service — which is a different problem than a rural coverage gap but still shows up as underservice in the data.

Reflections

The biggest thing I want to be honest about is that the FCC data this is built on has well-documented problems. The reason I used it anyway is that it is the only publicly available dataset that covers broadband availability at a sub-county level across the entire state — there is no alternative for statewide analysis. The FCC has been improving the data quality through its new challenge process and future versions of this tool would incorporate verified challenge results where available to give a more accurate picture of actual service.

I also think the Need Index weights I chose are defensible but not the only reasonable choice. The reason I made them adjustable was specifically because I knew that different users would have different priorities and I did not want the tool to impose my weighting on everyone who uses it. If I were building this for a specific state agency or grant program I would want to have a conversation with them about what the weights should be rather than treating my defaults as authoritative.

What I found most useful about this project is that it reframed broadband access as a compound problem — not just where the infrastructure is missing but where the missing infrastructure overlaps with communities that have the least capacity to absorb the gap. I think that framing is more actionable than a plain coverage map because it tells you not just where to build but why those places matter more than others.
