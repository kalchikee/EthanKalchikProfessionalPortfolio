Police Response Deserts

A spatial regression analysis testing whether Detroit police response times are longer in neighborhoods with higher proportions of non-white residents and lower incomes — mapping the geography of delayed emergency response.

Live Project ↗ ← Back to Portfolio

The Problem

Emergency response time is one of the most consequential variables in public safety — yet it is rarely mapped at the neighborhood level. Detroit, a city with a long history of fiscal stress and police department underfunding, provides a critical case study for examining whether response equity exists across its neighborhoods.

The central research question: Do Detroit police response times differ systematically by neighborhood race and income, even after controlling for call volume? If so, which neighborhoods wait the longest — and how much longer?

Methodology

1. Data Preparation

911 call records were filtered to Priority 1 (life-threatening) calls to control for call urgency. Response time was calculated as the difference between call receipt and officer arrival. Records with incomplete timestamps or geocoding errors were excluded.

2. Spatial Aggregation

Individual calls were spatially joined to Detroit’s 139 census tracts using point-in-polygon operations. Median response time was calculated per tract, weighted by call volume, to produce a single representative response time per tract.

3. Regression Analysis

An Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression was fit with tract-level median response time as the dependent variable and the following predictors:

VariableTypeSource
% Non-white residentsPredictorCensus ACS
Median household incomePredictorCensus ACS
Call volume (calls per 1k residents)ControlDetroit 911 CAD
Distance to nearest precinctControlTIGER/Line + Detroit GIS

4. Spatial Visualization

Regression residuals and predicted response times were mapped using Leaflet with a diverging color scale to highlight tracts with the largest deviations from expected response time.

Key Findings

Race is a statistically significant predictor (p < 0.05)

A 10-percentage-point increase in non-white tract population was associated with an average of 1.4 additional minutes of response time, holding income and call volume constant.

Income effect is stronger than race effect

A $10,000 decrease in median household income was associated with 2.1 additional minutes of response time — the largest single predictor in the model after controlling for precinct distance.

Northeast Detroit tracts wait longest

The 15 census tracts with the longest predicted response times (averaging 18+ minutes) are clustered in the northeast corner of the city — areas characterized by high poverty rates, low population density, and greater precinct distance.

Limitations & Future Work

This analysis is cross-sectional and cannot establish causation. Longer response times in low-income tracts may reflect patrol allocation decisions, officer deployment patterns, or geographic constraints rather than direct discrimination. A stronger design would include officer deployment data and shift-level controls.

Future work would apply spatial lag and spatial error regression models to account for spatial autocorrelation in response times across adjacent tracts, and extend the analysis to other Michigan cities for comparative context.
